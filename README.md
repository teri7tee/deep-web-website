## What is the Deep Web?

The deep web refers to the vast portion of the internet not indexed by standard search engines like Google. Unlike the surface web, which includes everyday sites like news portals and social media, the deep web encompasses content behind paywalls, private databases, and dynamic pages requiring authentication. In 2026, estimates suggest the deep web makes up over 90% of the total internet, hosting everything from academic journals to corporate intranets.

Deep Web Websites are accessed through specialized tools, but they aren't inherently malicious. Many serve legitimate purposes, such as secure government resources or medical records.

## Deep Web vs. Dark Web: Key Differences

### Understanding the Deep Web
The deep web is the non-indexed internet accessible via regular browsers with logins or specific URLs. Examples include email inboxes, online banking, and subscription services.

### The Dark Web Subset
The dark web is a small, encrypted part of the deep web, often requiring tools like Tor for anonymity. It's notorious for hidden services (.onion sites) that prioritize privacy. While it hosts whistleblower platforms and privacy-focused forums, it's also associated with illicit activities. In 2026, advancements in blockchain and VPN tech have blurred lines, but the dark web remains a hub for anonymous communication.
